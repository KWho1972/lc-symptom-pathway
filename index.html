RECOVER cluster — post-exertional
Your system is managing energy debt
Post-exertional malaise points to mitochondrial failure — your cellular power plants are damaged and can't recharge fast enough after use. Pushing past that limit depletes reserves that take days to rebuild. This isn't deconditioning. It's broken machinery trying to function anyway.
Do
  • Track activity against next-day symptoms to find your real threshold
  • Rest before you feel you need to, not after the crash
  • Eat breakfast before coffee — gives your cortisol accurate information
  • Prioritize sleep quality over duration
Avoid
  • Pushing through — activity that feels fine today can crash you tomorrow
  • Skipping meals or fasting protocols
  • High-intensity exercise until your threshold expands
  • Using good days as proof you've recovered

RECOVER cluster — cognitive
Your brain's signaling system is disrupted
Brain fog and emotional flatness in Long COVID often trace to dopamine suppression — the neurotransmitter that runs motivation, memory retrieval, and the felt sense of reward. When dopamine circuits are damaged, the words don't come and nothing feels worth doing. It's not depression. It's a disrupted signaling system.
Do
  • Protect sleep — dopamine pathways restore during REM
  • Small, achievable tasks to generate dopamine hits without overwhelm
  • Natural light exposure in the morning supports dopamine rhythm
  • Reduce decision load — cognitive tax depletes faster when signaling is impaired
Avoid
  • Multitasking or constant context-switching
  • Caffeine as a substitute for rest
  • Screens late at night — disrupts the dopamine-sleep cycle
  • Interpreting flatness as a personal failing

RECOVER cluster — autonomic
Your nervous system's regulator is stuck
Palpitations, dizziness on standing, and unusual thirst point to autonomic dysregulation — the part of your nervous system that manages heart rate, blood pressure, and fluid balance without your conscious involvement. It got recalibrated during illness and hasn't found its way back to baseline.
Do
  • Increase salt and fluid intake — autonomic dysfunction often involves low blood volume
  • Rise slowly from seated or lying positions
  • Consistent sleep and wake times — rhythm is regulating
  • Gentle horizontal movement (recumbent cycling, swimming) before upright exercise
Avoid
  • Hot showers or baths — vasodilation worsens orthostatic symptoms
  • Large meals — blood pools to digestion and away from the head
  • Standing still for long periods
  • Diuretics, including excess caffeine

RECOVER cluster — respiratory
Your airways are still in recovery mode
Chronic cough and breathlessness after COVID can reflect ongoing airway inflammation, hypersensitive respiratory nerves, or persistent immune activation in the lungs. The cough reflex gets sensitized — and once that happens, even normal triggers set it off. Your lungs aren't failing. They're hypervigilant.
Do
  • Nasal breathing over mouth breathing wherever possible
  • Slow, diaphragmatic breathing to calm the respiratory reflex
  • Avoid known irritants — smoke, strong scents, cold dry air
  • Track breathlessness against activity to find your current ceiling
Avoid
  • Forcefully suppressing the cough — can worsen hypersensitivity
  • Exertion that significantly raises breathing rate until stable
  • Assuming improvement is linear — respiratory recovery fluctuates

RECOVER cluster — sensory / olfactory
Your sensory pathways are slowly rewiring
Smell and taste changes — including parosmia (things smelling wrong) and phantom smells — reflect damage to olfactory neurons from viral entry. These neurons are among the few in the body capable of actual regeneration, which means this cluster often shows the most recovery over time. The process is slow and nonlinear, but it's real.
Do
  • Smell training — brief daily exposure to 4 distinct scents activates regeneration
  • Keep eating even when food tastes wrong — nutrition supports nerve repair
  • Document changes over time — improvement is often too gradual to feel day-to-day
Avoid
  • Concluding recovery isn't happening — olfactory regeneration takes 12–18+ months
  • Overpowering scents during acute parosmia phases
  • Skipping meals because food is unappealing — appetite may need rebuilding separately

RECOVER cluster — gastrointestinal
Your gut-brain connection is misfiring
Digestive symptoms in Long COVID often trace to three intersecting problems: depleted stomach acid (making nutrients unavailable even when eating well), damaged gut lining (triggering new food reactions), and disrupted vagal nerve signaling between gut and brain. You're not reacting to food. You're reacting to a gut that can no longer process it safely.
Do
  • Eat slowly in a calm state — the cephalic phase of digestion needs a signal before food arrives
  • Small, frequent meals over large ones
  • Bitter foods before meals (arugula, dandelion) to stimulate stomach acid
  • Track food reactions to distinguish sensitivity from timing or stress triggers
Avoid
  • Eating while stressed or distracted — shunts blood away from digestion
  • Long-term elimination diets without addressing gut lining
  • PPIs or acid reducers without medical guidance — low acid is more common than high in LC
  • Assuming food sensitivity is permanent
